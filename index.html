The platform that keeps the coffee from burning.

Most people think of Starbucks as ten thousand storefronts. The harder truth runs upstream — green beans on container ships, in customs, on roasters at four hundred and fifty degrees, in distribution centers waiting to move. A late alert anywhere along that chain can mean a quarter-million-dollar batch loss, a half-million-dollar damaged roaster, or a production halt that cascades to every store in North America. I designed and built the industrial-IoT alerting platform that protects all of it.

Bean burn prevention. Real-time roaster temperature thresholds, batch anomaly detection on color and aroma sensors, auto-shutdown triggers when roast profiles deviate, quality-control flags routed to roasting technicians. Every prevented incident saves roughly seven hundred and fifty thousand dollars in batch losses and roaster damage.

Supply-chain alerting. Port delays and customs holds on green-bean shipments. Temperature and moisture excursions inside shipping containers. Truck breakdowns rerouted in flight. Quality issues at origin farms, surfaced before crops cross the ocean. Distribution-center backlogs flagged before they became outages.

Underneath: nine thousand events per second sustained, forty thousand at peak, ingesting from millions of sensors across the value chain. On top: RESTful and GraphQL surfaces for ten thousand store managers and a ticketing layer for seventy thousand employees that cut support calls by twenty percent. End to end — design, architecture, build, test, delivery, and the design reviews that taught the next person to do it.

How I Work

  1. I

    Day-one delivery.

    Plans without ship dates are wishes. Cost reductions on day one beat roadmaps that promise them in the third quarter. The work is to find the smallest version of the right thing — and ship it before the meeting that would have debated it.

  2. II

    Outcomes over output.

    The unit of work is the metric that moved, not the ticket that closed. Every shipped thing should be answerable to a number — uptime, latency, dollars, hours. If it cannot be, it probably should not be shipped.

  3. III

    Automate the second time.

    The first manual run is reconnaissance. The second is a smell. By the third, the script should already exist. CI/CD, Terraform, and one good cron job have replaced more meetings than any process change ever has.

  4. IV

    Design for audit when it touches money.

    Traceability is not paperwork. It is the difference between a calm Monday and a regulator's letter. Every event with a financial consequence — every credit pull, every loan offer, every notification — should leave a record that survives the engineer who wrote it.

  5. V

    Compose intelligence, don't replace it.

    Agents are good at orchestration, not judgment. The leverage is in giving them grounded context and clear scopes — and trusting humans for the calls that matter. The best AI feature is the one that gives an engineer back an afternoon.

  6. VI

    See the whole picture.

    Whatever you build is part of getting something to a customer and a result to the business. Engineers who only focus on their component can make it better while making the whole worse. The most valuable work usually happens where one component feeds the next — like a credit report into a loan decision — not inside any single component.
